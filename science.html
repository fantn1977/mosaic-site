The Science

What Mosaic Measures — And Why

We didn't invent these signals. Exercise scientists did — some of them decades ago. We built Mosaic to surface them in a way that's actually useful. Here's what each one means.

Core Signal

The Mosaic Score

What it is: A single number — 0 to 100 — reflecting how close you are to your personal peak across all the signals Mosaic tracks.

Why it matters: Most health scores compare you to a population average. Your Mosaic Score compares you to you — specifically to the best version of yourself the data has observed. That's a fundamentally different question.

What moves it: Sleep quality, training load, recovery trends, resting heart rate. The ranked drivers below your score show you exactly which ones shifted — and by how much.

Mosaic surfaces this number. What it means for your day is yours to interpret.

Training Load

TRIMP — Training Impulse

What it is: A way of measuring how hard a workout actually was on your body. Not just how long. Not just how fast. How hard — weighted by intensity.

The plain English version: Thirty minutes at a casual pace is not the same as thirty minutes at your limit. TRIMP accounts for this. Time spent at high heart rate counts for significantly more than time spent at low heart rate — because that's how your body actually experiences effort.

The origin: Sports scientist Eric Banister developed this framework in the 1970s. It remains a gold standard for quantifying training stress because it reflects physiological reality, not just elapsed time.

Workload Balance

ACWR — Workload Ratio

What it is: A comparison between what you've done in the last 7 days versus your average over the last 4 weeks.

The plain English version: Your body adapts to training over time. Your 4-week average represents what it's currently prepared for. Your 7-day load represents what you're asking of it right now. The ratio between them is a signal worth watching.

What the research suggests: A ratio in the moderate range generally reflects training within your current capacity. Ratios significantly above that range have been associated with increased injury risk in athletic populations.

Recovery

Recovery Status

What it is: A reading of how well your body is bouncing back — based on overnight heart rate patterns, heart rate variability signals, and sleep data.

Why it matters: Training stress and life stress both cost your body something. Recovery Status reflects how well that bill is being paid. A body that isn't recovering isn't adapting — it's accumulating.

This is a signal, not a diagnosis. Many things affect recovery. Mosaic tracks the ones your devices can measure.

Your Baseline

Your Personal Baseline

What it is: Not a chart average. Not a population benchmark. The peak that your data has actually observed — across all sources, over time.

Why it matters: Knowing you scored a 72 means almost nothing without context. Knowing you scored a 72 when your personal peak is 85 — and that you've been trending away from it for 11 days — means something.

How Mosaic builds it: Mosaic identifies your best sustained performance windows across your health history and uses them as the reference point for everything else. It updates as you change.

Your baseline is yours. It will evolve as you do.

A note on what Mosaic doesn't do

Mosaic is not a medical device. It doesn't diagnose conditions, prescribe training plans, or tell you what your numbers mean for your health. These signals were selected because they have legitimate research foundations in exercise science and recovery physiology. We surface them because we believe people deserve to see real data about their own bodies — not just a colored circle and a mood emoji.

What you do with that information is up to you.
